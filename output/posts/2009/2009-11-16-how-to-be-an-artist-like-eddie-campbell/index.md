"The simplest most everyday things are better than created extravaganzas..."

**2009 ComICA Festival, London.**  The session with Eddie Campbell at the Institute of Contemporary Arts began at 7 and I was running late. (A long story involving a broken printer, Tube closures, busted trains, Neanderthal passengers, a wailing baby, a tsunami of Piccadilly Circus tourists and a hundred meter dash up numerous flights of stairs. If I had half the brilliance of Mr. Campbell, I would show all this in a chuckle-inducing 9-panel spread, but I digress.) By the time I make it to the lecture room, witty banter’s in full swing as high caliber Glaswegian comic book artist gets grilled by high caliber Glaswegian comic. It couldn’t be more apt.

So there he was, Eddie Campbell, Alec MacGarry’ himself(!), in the flesh (plain undershirt, salt 'n' pepper hair). Having the piss taken out of him by comedian Arnold Brown for name-dropping Quentin Tarantino’s mum who once commissioned the former to sketch her own Bacchus, god of wine.  (Eddie:  Connie, yeah, she gets around…Arnie:  My God, how famous!)

The main thread of the conversation here of course, would be the launch of Campbell’s mega-collection, Alec:  The Years Have Pants. A hefty 640-pager that brings together his semi-autobiographical Alec books–The King Canute Crowd, Graffiti Kitchen, How to be an Artist, Little Italy, The Dead Muse, The Dance of Lifey Death,  After the Snooter and some previously unpublished fragments plus a new book that bears the compilation’s title. Aldo had given me this wonderful omnibus as a birthday present and I was chuffed to lug it all the way to Central London to have it signed.

Back in the early Nineties, I had stumbled upon Campbell’s autobiographical Alec stories while rummaging through Luis Katigbak's incredible stash of Book Sale finds. Barely held together by a Scotch-taped plastic bag, stripped of covers, and looking the worse for wear, those pages of candid, humorous stories brought to life with doodle-like yet quite refined drawings of Alec making funny faces with his new baby certainly caught my fancy. The storytelling was light and yet it tugged at the heartstrings. Not long after that, I came across Mr. Campbell’s name again as he gained more recognition for working with Alan Moore on From Hell. And by the time discussions on the graphic novel and sequential art ensued on local fanboy mailing lists, anyone with the money could soon get spanking-new, even signed copies of Campbell’s Top Shelf releases imported by high-end bookstores in Manila.

Anyway, back to the conversation in London. If I had to tell you some highlights of what my muddled brain picked up from this whole funny, inspiring event (aside from a prized autograph and sketch, of course) it would all really boil down to these:

Some tips on how to be an artist like Eddie Campbell

**1.    Unassuming material is good material.**

Arnold Brown interrogates Campbell about the authenticity of his characters and stories.   Is there a real Danny Grey? Did he really leave you sleeping at the turnpike (as in the early _King Canute_ comics)? We can easily guess the answer to that. Campbell nods and later cites more instances of art imitating life–in one of them he is fearless and twenty-three, pissing on the Thames from a rubber dinghy.

Later, he shares that the $4000 he paid a travel agent for his recent Italian trip somehow got siphoned off, credit-crunch style, before the actual ticket was procured.  “There’s a story angle there and that would be good for my next book which is going to be all about money - the awful, horrid stuff!”

How do you create things to write about? Someone in the audience asks Campbell who answers that there is no need (for him) to create things to write about. “Unassuming material is good indeed. The simplest most everyday things are better than created extravaganzas. Waking up in the middle of the night in cold sweat - now that already offers a million possibilities.”

**2.   Imagine you are the Duke of Wellington.**

Just as it was for his alter ego Alec, (in _King Canute #1_) formal education was an obstacle to Eddie Campbell's artistic progress. He had quit his foundation course at what was then known as the Central School of Art and Design in London, and then spent five years in a factory, cutting sheet metal into squares.

Did you take the job as an artist’s decision? Brown asks. Apparently, he did and the mindlessness of the job left him lots of room to read and ruminate on logic and mythology. “The important thing is that to be an artist you have to imagine you are one already.”  He smiles. “I thought I was a successful artist.  If anyone pointed it out that I wasn’t, I’d be crestfallen. In Glasgow, when I was 6, I thought I was a cowboy–not that I wanted to be one (mind,) but that I was one. To survive, you have got to be mad.  You have got to not notice you’re not getting respect. You gotta be oblivious to that. I thought I was the Duke of Wellington.”

**3.  Sometimes the dining table can be better than a studio.**

A guy sitting across the aisle asks, “Do you still do your work on the dining table?”  Campbell shrugs and confesses that these days, he spends more work time in front of the computer. But he does wax nostalgic about that long kitchen table he used to toil on.  There was a demarcation line, he recalls, but paintbrushes would fall over the eating area. Zip-a-tones (those now-obsolete flat sticky sheets with dots and patterns used for shading in comics) were all over the place so much that. “If you walked around the house they would end up in the crack of your bum…heck and on the baby–in the folds of his neck.”

“How can you work in this madness?” his wife had exclaimed. Eventually she convinced him to go work in a studio, sans the bedlam and the kids. And boy was it quiet in that studio.  Campbell laughs. “And what would you do in that silence?  I read a book.”

“I need the noise to work.  All that alien information is good for creative thinking.  Natural thinking, as Edward de Bono calls it. (“The dullest stuff!  Self-help books.  Shouldn’t everyone think in the same way you used to think when you were three?”  On that note, Brown brings up Malcolm Gladwell’s The Outliers and his notion of devoting ten years to master a craft.)

**4.    The god of wine is in the details.**

Brown praises Campbell’s eye for detail. “You have a laser eye – not a lazy eye.” And talk of technique ensues.  “The artist mind locks information in. Mentally sketching all the time.” Campbell says like a sage. Contrary to viewing cartoons as (simplified images) distilled from reality, Campbell shares that in painting Bacchus (the bespoke one for Tarantino’s mum) he had a really hard time getting the hair and whiskers right. How would you draw the hair of someone who was 4000 years old?  (In the end, he, uh, reveals that what he used for reference was pubes.)

As for them zip-a-tones (which you can still find in Japan) he says someone sent him some and that he is “using it in a painterly way, like blobs of toothpaste. It looks juicy and painterly–that’s the word we artists like.”

The cover of _The Years Have Pants_ depicts Alec lying on grass-like scraps of Zip-a-tone.  The guy seated beside me comments that the cover reminds him of a funeral. “As if I float off to Avalon!”  Campbell light up.  Incidentally, he tells us that the cover designer is Eric Skillman who also designs Criterion Collection DVDs.

**5.  A little humour won’t hurt**

"How would you know what to tell and what not to tell?" A guy at the back row enquires. Campbell says it was never really a major problem. He never got into deep trouble with friends or family on how they were being depicted in his stories. _The Jester_, the fool in society is after all, allowed to tell the truth, he said. He mentions Joe Sacco, another graphic novelist whom he thinks epitomises just that. “Now he is a handsome chap but always depicts himself as a foolish-looking character in order to talk about the Palestine and Israel conflict....The mix of humour in comics develops into the very root of what we are. Sacco can get away with tackling delicate situations regular journalists cannot talk about.

**6.  Appreciate your peers**

Is there anyone out there that you think might push the media further? Someone in the audience asks. Campbell says he likes Craig Thompson, especially for his new book _Carnet de Voyage_, about his travels in Europe. He also mentions that he likes how the _Blankets_ author draws figures "with lovely big feet."  Too bad someone told him to draw them smaller.

Another new favourite is Robert Crumb’s _Genesis_. He singles out the part in which the Household of Abraham was told to get a circumcision and how in that one, Crumb’s eyes kept looking surreptitiously from side to side, as if to say, Do we have an option here?

Everyone laughs.

**7.     Keep on working and collaborating**

Eddie Campbell’s next project is _The Playwright_, written by Darren White, to be published by Top Shelf and Knockabout next year.  “It’s about the sex life of a celibate middle-aged man and there’s a lot of self-abuse in it.  Critics said ‘it’s not worth reading, it’s just full of wanking." We said–that should be the blurb!”

You seem quite happy, Brown observes aloud to the artist, who doesn’t disagree. Ah, the actual _Dance of Lifey Death_. Eddie Campbell must know himself very well:  “Youth, old age, perfection, flaws, it’s all there in a big soup–a big **Campbellian** soup.”
