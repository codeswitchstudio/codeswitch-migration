> For ![425792_10150670320732814_1790416405_n](images/425792_10150670320732814_1790416405_n-300x238.jpg)the artist, love of the work has to come first.  Art is not a soft option, it is a lifetime's struggle. --**Jeanette Winterson**, _Books n Riches_        

When I first got a glimpse of _My Mother and Me_ (acrylic and collage on canvas) in 2006, it became an instant favourite. It was both so like and unlike [Imelda Cajipe-Endaya's](https://lifestyle.inquirer.net/259619/imelda-cajipe-endaya-woman-artist-seasons/) previous works. Windows, textures, stark color contrasts. The living room of the house of holiday visits turned temporary shelter in Newburgh is depicted here. It is empty but with telltale signs of lives lived fully. A basket of yarn and a cane rest by the sofa. Snow-encrusted tree branches can be glimpsed outside. A cold winter in a place so far away from home is countered by the cheering presence of flowers. A meta-picture wall hanging of my mother as a small child, looking shyly at the camera and her own mother, my grandmother, young and smiling.

Although I have yet to see the more recent studio where she had painted this, I can imagine what it is like. Growing up accustomed to the smell of Grumbacher and linseed oil in the morning, I’ve observed how mama’s materials and media changed with the passing of time as she embarked on her artistic journeys: from her prints, to her canvasses and collages, to her larger-than-life oil-paintings and then the tiny delicate acrylic works on handmade paper, to her sculptures, watercolors, installations, digital art, organizing work, and curatorships.

If there’s anything I learned early on in life, it’s how not to be afraid to be untidy and get one’s hands dirty. The San Juan studio in the Seventies was a clunky clutter of iron plates, etching knives, paint rollers, a plexiglass acid bath tray to clean the plates, and a heavy metal etching press straight out of Victorian steampunk novels. It was the time her works like _Piko_ (a Filipino version of hopscotch), Forefathers, _Hinog sa Araw_  (Sun-ripened) came out. Pretty good stimuli for developing one’s sense of wonder.

The studio was never off-limits to me as creating art and rearing children had neither separate work hours nor clearly-defined breaks. Apparently, I was also allotted a work area of my own. This is documented in an old slide, which shows a picture of me with my own little wooden blue table, my hands covered with flour from homemade Play-Doh or “Clay” dough. (Evidence from the same slide also shows that early on, a career in sculpture was not in the offing for me.)

Towards the Eighties, we moved to our own place in Parañaque. Despite being blessed with a spacious yard with a nipa play-hut and lots of trees, mama’s studio was still a fascinating space for us kids. It was the coolest place at the bottom of the house and from the concrete beams of the unfinished ceiling, my folks hung an indoor swing for my little sister. We even had our own children’s work desks that doubled up as my pretend-detective agency office.

And yet, it was a serious creative workspace which housed the aforementioned tubes of oil paint, along with tin cans full of paint brushes, vats and vats full of carpenters glue, finishes, varnishes and fixatives.

Now that I think about it, strict borders never existed at home. The whole house was a studio with mounted canvasses that would stretch across the entire dining area floor.

There were swatches and patches all over:  Lola Minggay’s (my great grandma‘s) crocheted doilies, veils and scapulars, Time magazine covers with missiles of the Reagan years, kitschy table cloths with bright red apples and flowers, old jeans, red papier mache horses from Paete. Pieces meant to be discarded, but brought to life with new meaning: Jesus as a laborer in jeans, a handmade clothes dummy on a used ironing board, a plaster-cast hand coming out of a broken microwave oven, art from a damaged computer mother board. There was also a collection of news clippings wars in the Middle East, the EDSA revolution, and Filipino diasporas (men in Saudi Arabia, women in Hongkong.)

Thus, another thing I learned early on was not to throw anything away as they could be either material or stimuli. Also then, I was already grasping how my mother’s art was a reaction to the times, as much as a reaction to society, domesticity and gender roles.

How does it feel to be the daughter of an artist? I’ve been asked. Not too different from the daughter of a dentist or a housewife or a grocer, I would have wanted to believe.

In hindsight though, I must admit that mama's rearing practice was a tinge more experimental, post-colonial, non-consumerist, and non-conformist.

It was an early childhood of no Barbies and no junk food. There was gallery-hopping instead of shopping. This was before the art galleries were conveniently located in malls. I remember the City Gallery in Luneta, also hanging out as a little kid at the loft of the Hiraya Gallery in Malate which is still around but feels smaller because we’ve grown up. There were exhibits and some short-lived disappointments for the lack of red dots (stickers that meant “sold) because non-ornamental, non-escapist, social-realist paintings never really sell like hot cakes at gallery openings.

Funnily, it was only my university years that I met people who more or less shared the same kind of background but they were more bohemian, more confident, less hesitant to express themselves, whether in [art](https://aaa.org.hk/archive/249013), music or in writing.

For a while, I did try to hide this side of myself while trying to fit into my Catholic private school for high school girls in Manila. I remember being an insecure adolescent worried about what classmates would think of our bedroom walls which, although they were painted red blue and yellow like the Philippine flag, were filled with Crayola graffiti, as my sister and I were free to doodle on them. Also, all our heavy books at home–dictionaries, the Britannica, the Encyclopedias of Western Artists, had a thin coat of dried-up white glue on the dust covers because they were used to weigh down the mixed media mounted on the canvasses.

Then again, a friend found it fascinating that the bulky black electric transformers at home were deliberately splotched with pink and yellow paint. A visual flaw morphed into something interesting. I’d like to think of it as my mother’s approach: this growing into uniqueness and embracing it but not being so full of the self.

My mother tells me that when I was a very small child, I thought it was the duty of all mothers to make art. Perhaps this is because she sensibly nurtures her art as a part of life instead of wrapping it up with the stereotypical artiste’s ego and putting it on a cocktail party-festooned pedestal.

The life of any artist could be easily romanticized by words but my mother was never self-conscious about being an 'artiste' and never had airs or a sense of entitlement after [achieving so much](https://theartling.com/en/artzine/artist-defined-contemporary-southeast-asian-art/). It was pure love of the work, no flamboyant diva ego, just being really professional, as one of the gallery owners told me before. There was just too much to do: she would just move on to the next project or help another group of people with theirs. Making more room for more art to thrive.

Now that I am miles away from home and far from all that is comforting and familiar, I find myself buying four little framed canvases, brushes, a palette knife, and some tubes of bright acrylic. When I set them up in the room, in an organized sort of clutter, I feel more at home. Some kind of nostalgia for mama’s studio.
